## Understanding the Trade War Spectrum

A global trade war differs from bilateral disputes (e.g., US/China) in that it involves broad tariff escalation against multiple trading partners simultaneously — including traditional allies such as Canada, Mexico, the EU, Japan, and South Korea — combined with retaliatory measures, supply chain fragmentation, and a potential breakdown of the rules-based trading system anchored by the WTO. The key investment variable is the degree to which inflation is imported through tariffs and the extent to which corporate supply chains must be restructured at significant cost.

| Level | Description | Example Events |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **1 – Routine Friction** | Targeted tariffs on specific goods; WTO dispute resolution functioning | Section 201/232 tariffs on steel and aluminum; targeted agricultural tariffs |
| **2 – Broad Tariff Escalation** | Sweeping tariffs on multiple categories across multiple trading partners | 25%+ tariffs on imports from Canada, Mexico, EU simultaneously; blanket tariff threats |
| **3 – Retaliation Underway** | Multiple major trading partners imposing retaliatory tariffs on US exports | EU tariffs on US agricultural products, autos; Canadian counter-tariffs; Japanese retaliation |
| **4 – WTO Breakdown / Trade Blocs** | Collapse of multilateral trade framework; world fragmenting into regional trade blocs | US withdrawal from WTO dispute resolution; bilateral deal negotiations replacing multilateral rules |
| **5 – Full Trade Fragmentation** | Comprehensive trade barriers; deglobalization embedded; permanent supply chain restructuring | Autarky-adjacent policies; full supply chain reshoring; end of global just-in-time manufacturing |

## Key Risk Factors to Monitor

### Policy & Legislative
- US Trade Representative (USTR) Section 201, 232, and 301 tariff announcements and Federal Register filings
- Executive orders invoking International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) for tariff imposition
- Congressional reaction — bipartisan opposition to tariffs can constrain executive action
- WTO dispute panel filing and ruling timeline (18–36 month process)
- Trading partner retaliatory tariff list announcements (EU, Canada, Japan, Mexico)

### Trade & Economic Data
- US trade deficit data (monthly Census Bureau release) — politicians use as justification for tariffs
- Import price indices by category (BLS) — measures inflation pass-through from tariffs
- US Export data by category — identifies which sectors face retaliation risk
- Manufacturing PMI (new orders and export orders components) — deterioration indicates trade war damage
- Business investment (capex) intentions surveys — supply chain uncertainty delays investment

### Supply Chain & Corporate
- Shipping rate indices (Drewry, Baltic Dry) — trade volume declines as tariff uncertainty rises
- Port congestion and container demand data (pull-forward and then collapse pattern)
- Company earnings calls — management discussion of tariff exposure and mitigation strategies
- Gross margin trends in tariff-exposed sectors (autos, electronics, consumer goods)
- Reshoring/nearshoring announcements — Mexico (nearshoring beneficiary), US industrial real estate

### Currency
- USD DXY Index — tariffs are theoretically USD-positive (reduced import demand), but uncertainty offsets
- CNY/USD (separate from US/China guide but amplified in global trade war)
- MXN, CAD, EUR, KRW — directly affected currencies when their countries face US tariffs
- Trading partner central bank responses to export sector pressure

## Sector Playbook

### Overweight in Trade War Escalation

| Sector | Rationale | Representative Names |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **US Domestic-Revenue Industrials** | Benefit from tariff protection and reshoring demand; minimal import cost exposure | CAT (selective), DE (domestic sales), ITW, PCAR |
| **US Steel & Aluminum Producers** | Section 232 tariff protection directly benefits domestic metals producers | NUE, STLD, CLF, AA, CENX |
| **US Onshoring / Factory Construction** | Capital spending surge on domestic manufacturing buildout | MTZ, PWR, EMN, MLM, VMC |
| **US Defense & Aerospace** | Domestic production, government contracts; minimal tariff exposure on revenue | LMT, RTX, NOC, GD |
| **Domestic-Only Consumer Staples** | Revenue entirely US-based; no export retaliation risk; tariff pass-through ability | WMT, COST, KR, DG (domestic operations) |
| **Energy Infrastructure** | Domestic US energy; LNG as export to tariff-affected countries remains valuable | LNG, KMI, WMB, CQP |
| **Discount Retail** | Trade-down as tariff inflation erodes consumer purchasing power | WMT, DLTR, DG, COST |
| **Gold & Safe Havens** | Uncertainty premium; flight to safety when trade conflict triggers growth fears | GLD, PHYS, SHV |

### Underweight / Reduce in Trade War Escalation

| Sector | Rationale | Representative Names |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **US Multinationals — Export Dependent** | Retaliatory tariffs directly hit revenue; supply chain cost increases | CAT (export), DE (export), BA (commercial), AGCO |
| **US Autos & Auto Parts** | Complex cross-border supply chains; assembled vehicles and parts face tariffs from both sides | F, GM, STLA, LEA, MGA |
| **Consumer Electronics** | Assembly in tariff-affected countries; cost pass-through damages demand | AAPL, HPQ, DELL, SNE |
| **Apparel & Footwear** | Manufacturing concentration in tariff-affected countries | NKE, LEVI, HBI, VFC, UAA |
| **Agricultural Exporters** | Retaliatory tariffs specifically target US soybeans, corn, pork — key export markets | ADM, BG, INGR, SAFM |
| **Luxury Consumer Goods** | European and Asian luxury faces counter-tariffs; US luxury faces retaliation | RL, EL, TPR, CPRI |
| **Global Logistics & Shipping** | Trade volume declines with tariff escalation; revenue linked to global trade flows | FDX, UPS, ZIM, MATX, DAC |
| **Broad Emerging Markets** | Export-dependent economies suffer from global trade contraction | EEM, VWO (particularly Mexico, South Korea, Taiwan, Vietnam) |

## Individual Stock Considerations

### Long Candidates

**US Steel & Metals (Tariff-Protected):**
- **NUE (Nucor)** – Largest US steel producer; electric arc furnace technology (lower cost); direct beneficiary of Section 232 tariff protection; disciplined capital allocation
- **STLD (Steel Dynamics)** – Fastest-growing US steelmaker; lower-cost mini-mill model; flat rolled steel for domestic auto and appliance demand
- **AA (Alcoa)** – Largest US aluminum producer; Section 232 tariff on aluminum imports directly benefits margins and pricing power

**Domestic Industrial / Onshoring:**
- **MLM (Martin Marietta Materials)** – Aggregates for domestic construction; entirely US revenue; reshoring factory construction is a major growth driver
- **VMC (Vulcan Materials)** – Largest US aggregates producer; zero tariff exposure; infrastructure construction beneficiary

**Defense:**
- **LMT (Lockheed Martin)** – US government revenue; no export tariff risk; defense spending uncorrelated with trade policy
- **NOC (Northrop Grumman)** – Similar profile; domestic revenue dominance

### Short / Avoid Candidates

- **AAPL (Apple)** – ~80% of iPhone assembly in tariff-affected countries; cost pass-through risks demand; China market retaliation risk
- **NKE (Nike)** – Footwear manufacturing in Vietnam, Indonesia, China; tariff cost structure exposure; Asian market retaliatory consumer boycott risk
- **ADM / BG (agricultural exporters)** – Retaliatory tariffs on US soybeans, corn, and wheat are the first lever trading partners pull; export revenue collapses
- **CAT (Caterpillar) — export-focused segments** – Construction equipment exports face retaliatory tariffs; global mining equipment faces reciprocal measures

## Hedging Strategies

### Inflation and Cost Hedges

1. **Long XME (metals and mining ETF)** — Steel and aluminum price spikes from tariff-restricted imports benefit domestic producers; XME captures this via NUE, STLD, AA, CLF
2. **Long TIPS (TIP, VTIP)** — Tariff-driven inflation is imported; TIPS protect against CPI increases from cost pass-through; hold in tax-deferred accounts (phantom income)
3. **Long DBC (broad commodities)** — Tariff disruptions create commodity price dislocations; commodity basket provides broad inflation hedge

### Currency Hedges

- **Long USD (UUP)** — Trade deficits narrow with tariffs → less USD supply sent abroad → USD appreciation; also, flight to safety in uncertainty
- **Short CAD (FXC puts)** — Canada is the largest US trading partner; direct tariff exposure weakens CAD
- **Short MXN via EWW puts** — Mexico is deeply integrated with US manufacturing; tariff disruption hits MXN hard
- **Short EUR (FXE puts)** — EU retaliatory tariffs hurt European export sectors; ECB may ease in response, weakening EUR

### Equity Hedges

1. **EEM put spreads** — Emerging market equity falls as global trade contracts; defined-risk downside on EM basket
2. **QQQ put spreads** — Tech multinationals face both supply chain cost inflation and revenue risk from foreign retaliation
3. **XRT puts** — Consumer discretionary retail faces both import cost increases and consumer spending pressure from tariff-driven inflation
4. **SPY put spreads** — Systemic economic slowdown hedge for global trade war escalation

### Tax-Efficiency Considerations

- **US steel/metals stocks (NUE, STLD, AA) in taxable accounts** — If held over 1 year, qualify for preferential long-term capital gains rates; relatively high dividend yields generate ordinary income but dividends from US C-corps qualify for qualified dividend treatment (0%/15%/20%)
- **Currency ETF options (FXC, FXE puts)** — Currency options are generally Section 988 ordinary income instruments by default; consult a tax advisor about Section 1256 treatment options to access the 60/40 blended rate
- **Commodity ETFs (DBC, XME)** — DBC uses Section 1256 mark-to-market (60% long-term / 40% short-term regardless of holding period); XME is an equity ETF with standard capital gains treatment
- **TIPS in tax-deferred accounts** — Phantom income on inflation accrual is ordinary income in taxable accounts; always hold TIPS in IRA/401k to defer this tax drag
- **EEM, EWW, EWC put options** — These are equity options taxed as short-term capital gains if held less than 12 months; time exits strategically; note that losses on short positions and puts are short-term by default
- **Tax-loss harvesting** — Trade war selloffs in tech (QQQ), retail (XRT), and international equity (EWW, EWC, EWY) create harvesting opportunities; swap between similar ETFs (e.g., sell EWW and buy iShares MSCI Mexico Small-Cap) to maintain exposure while realizing the loss

## Scenario-Based Positioning

### Scenario A: Broad Tariff Escalation (Level 2)

**Probability driver:** USTR announces sweeping tariffs on multiple countries simultaneously; no prior negotiation; effective immediately or within 90 days

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Add XME, NUE, STLD | Domestic steel/aluminum direct beneficiaries of tariff protection |
| Trim AAPL, NKE, ADM | Immediate cost and revenue exposure |
| Add GLD 3–5% | Uncertainty and inflation hedge |
| Add TIP/VTIP 5% | Tariff-driven inflation hedge; hold in IRA |
| Buy EWW, EWC puts (3-month, OTM) | Canada and Mexico face direct tariff exposure |
| Long USD (UUP) 3% | Dollar typically strengthens on tariff announcement |

### Scenario B: Retaliation Underway (Level 3)

**Probability driver:** EU, Canada, Mexico, Japan announce retaliatory tariff lists specifically targeting US exports; agricultural sector under pressure

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Eliminate agricultural exporters (ADM, BG) | Retaliatory tariffs destroy export market revenue directly |
| Add domestic construction/reshoring (MLM, VMC, IFRA) | Reshoring manufacturing requires domestic factory buildout |
| Increase TIPS allocation to 15% (in IRA) | Inflation entrenching across multiple supply chains |
| Trim multinational tech (AAPL, QQQ) 10% | Foreign market retaliation risk compounds supply chain cost increases |
| Add XLP, WMT, COST | Trade-down consumption; domestic revenue base |
| Buy SPY put spreads (3–6 month) | Economic slowdown from trade friction; earnings estimates need to come down |

### Scenario C: WTO Breakdown / Trade Bloc Formation (Level 4)

**Probability driver:** US withdraws from WTO dispute panels; bilateral deal negotiations replace multilateral rules; world fragmenting into regional blocs

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Maximum domestic-only equity exposure | Multinationals face structural revenue and cost headwinds; domestic businesses insulated |
| Increase gold and commodities (GLD, DBC) to 15% | Hard assets preserve value in fragmented, inflationary trade environment |
| Eliminate broad emerging markets (EEM, VWO) | Global trade contraction devastates export-dependent EM economies |
| Add energy infrastructure (KMI, LNG) | Domestic US energy; LNG exports valued by tariff-disrupted allies |
| Add US defense (ITA) | Defense spending rises as geopolitical fragmentation follows economic fragmentation |
| Maximum TIPS allocation (IRA) | Structural inflation embedding from permanent supply chain restructuring |

### Scenario D: Trade Deal / De-escalation (Level 1–2)

**Probability driver:** Bilateral framework agreements announced; tariff pause or reduction; WTO dispute resolution re-engaged; retaliatory measures suspended

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Trim steel/metals (NUE, XME) | Tariff protection premium deflates on deal announcement |
| Re-add multinationals (AAPL, QQQ) | Tariff cost relief + market reopening sentiment |
| Re-add emerging markets (EEM, EWW, EWC) | Relief trade; export-dependent economies recover fastest |
| Reduce TIPS / inflation hedges | Trade-driven inflation eases with tariff reduction |
| Reduce gold | Risk-on; uncertainty premium deflates |
| Add agricultural exporters (ADM, BG) | Export markets reopen; retaliatory tariffs lifted |

## Key Indicators & Triggers

### Early Warning Signals (Begin Defensive Positioning)
- USTR Federal Register notice of new Section 232, 301, or IEEPA tariff investigation covering multiple major trading partners
- Presidential announcement of blanket tariff rates (e.g., "universal baseline tariff") before formal process
- Trading partner official statements warning of "proportional retaliation" within 30 days
- ISM Manufacturing export orders component declining below 48 for two consecutive months
- Business Roundtable CEO Economic Outlook Survey declining sharply; uncertainty cited as primary factor

### Escalation Confirmation Signals (Full Defensive Positioning)
- Effective implementation of broad tariffs above 15% on major trading partners (Canada, EU, Mexico)
- Trading partner retaliatory tariff lists published targeting US agricultural exports and manufactured goods
- US agricultural export data declining more than 20% year-over-year (monthly Census Bureau)
- Manufacturing PMI declining below 45; new orders component collapsing
- Multinational company earnings guidance cuts citing tariff cost increases above 2% of revenue

### De-escalation / Deal Signals (Reduce Defensive Positioning)
- Joint statement from US and major trading partner announcing tariff suspension or phase-out timeline
- USTR initiating formal negotiation process with specific deal framework announced
- Trading partner retaliatory tariff suspension or pause announced
- ISM Manufacturing new export orders recovering above 50 for two consecutive months
- Shipping volume indices recovering; container rates normalizing

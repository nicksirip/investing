## Understanding the Recession Spectrum

US recessions vary significantly in depth and duration. Positioning should shift gradually as economic conditions evolve across the cycle.

| Level | Description | Example Events |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **1 – Expansion** | Healthy growth, low unemployment | GDP >2%, unemployment <4%, credit spreads tight |
| **2 – Slowdown** | Growth decelerating, early warning signs | GDP <1%, yield curve inversion, declining PMI |
| **3 – Mild Recession** | Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP | Rising unemployment, tightening credit, earnings misses |
| **4 – Deep Recession** | Severe contraction, high unemployment | Unemployment >7%, credit freeze, widespread layoffs |
| **5 – Depression / Systemic Crisis** | Multi-year contraction, financial system stress | Unemployment >10%, bank failures, deflation risk |

## Key Risk Factors to Monitor

### Macroeconomic Indicators
- GDP growth rate (quarterly BEA release)
- Unemployment rate and initial jobless claims (weekly DOL release)
- ISM Manufacturing and Services PMI (monthly)
- Consumer Confidence Index (Conference Board, monthly)
- Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) and retail sales

### Federal Reserve & Credit Markets
- Federal Funds Rate trajectory (FOMC meeting decisions and dot plot)
- Yield curve shape — 10Y/2Y and 10Y/3M spreads (inversion = recession signal)
- Investment-grade and high-yield credit spreads (FRED data)
- Bank lending standards (Federal Reserve Senior Loan Officer Survey, quarterly)
- Mortgage delinquency and default rates

### Financial / Market Signals
- S&P 500 earnings growth and forward guidance revisions
- VIX Index (sustained elevation above 25 indicates market stress)
- High-yield bond ETF (HYG) price relative to investment-grade (LQD)
- Banking sector performance (KBE, KRE) relative to broad market
- Housing starts and building permits (monthly Census Bureau release)

## Sector Playbook

### Overweight in Recessionary Scenarios

| Sector | Rationale | Representative Names |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Consumer Staples** | Inelastic demand; resilient revenue through economic cycles | PG, KO, PEP, COST, WMT |
| **Healthcare** | Non-discretionary spending; defensive earnings profile | JNJ, UNH, ABT, CVS, MDT |
| **Utilities** | Regulated, predictable cash flows; dividend stability | NEE, DUK, SO, AEP, D |
| **US Treasuries & Investment-Grade Bonds** | Flight to safety; price appreciation as Fed cuts rates | TLT, IEF, AGG, BND |
| **Gold & Hard Assets** | Store of value; uncorrelated to equity drawdowns | GLD, IAU, PHYS, NEM |
| **Discount Retailers** | Trade-down effect as consumers cut spending | WMT, DLTR, DG, COST |
| **Dividend Aristocrats** | Stable income; historically lower drawdowns | NOBL, SDY, VIG |

### Underweight / Reduce in Recessionary Scenarios

| Sector | Rationale | Representative Names |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Consumer Discretionary** | Cyclical demand falls sharply as consumers cut spending | AMZN (retail), TGT, MCD, NKE, SBUX |
| **Financials (Banks & Regional)** | Loan loss provisions rise; net interest margin compression | BAC, C, JPM, KEY, RF |
| **Industrials & Materials** | Demand collapses with business investment | CAT, DE, NUE, FCX, AA |
| **Energy (Oil & Gas E&P)** | Demand destruction; oil price declines in recessions | XOM, CVX, OXY, PXD |
| **Small Caps** | Higher debt costs; weaker balance sheets; credit access impaired | IWM, IJR |
| **High-Yield / Junk Bonds** | Default rates spike in recessions | HYG, JNK |
| **Real Estate (Commercial REITs)** | Vacancy rises; refinancing risk in high-rate environment | VNQ (selectively), SLG, BXP |

## Individual Stock Considerations

### Long Candidates

**Consumer Staples:**
- **PG (Procter & Gamble)** – Household and personal care products; pricing power and global distribution; consistently raised dividend for 60+ consecutive years
- **KO (Coca-Cola)** – Global beverage brand; resilient through every US recession; long dividend growth history
- **WMT (Walmart)** – Benefits from trade-down effect; grocery and essentials dominance; scale advantage

**Healthcare:**
- **JNJ (Johnson & Johnson)** – Diversified pharma and MedTech; fortress balance sheet; AAA credit rating
- **UNH (UnitedHealth Group)** – Managed care; recession-resistant insurance premiums

**Utilities:**
- **NEE (NextEra Energy)** – Largest US utility; regulated earnings base plus renewable growth pipeline
- **DUK (Duke Energy)** – Regulated utility serving the Southeast; predictable rate base earnings

**Fixed Income / Treasuries:**
- **TLT** – 20+ year Treasury ETF; typically rallies as Fed cuts rates in recessions; core defensive holding

### Short / Avoid Candidates

- **Consumer discretionary retail (TGT, SBUX, NKE)** – Wallet-share loss as consumer budgets tighten
- **Regional banks (KEY, RF, ZION)** – Loan loss exposure, NIM compression, deposit flight risk
- **High-yield issuers with heavy debt loads** – Refinancing risk as credit conditions tighten
- **Small and micro-cap growth companies** – Lack of profitability becomes existential in credit crunch

## Hedging Strategies

### Options-Based Hedges

1. **SPY put spreads** – Buy ATM put / sell OTM put; defined-risk hedge on broad market drawdown; roll quarterly
2. **IWM puts** – Small caps typically lead the market lower in recessions; leverage drawdown exposure
3. **HYG put options** – Credit canary; HYG break signals broader credit deterioration
4. **KRE put options** – Regional bank stress can be a leading indicator of systemic risk
5. **XHB puts** – Homebuilder ETF; housing is one of the most rate-sensitive sectors

### Interest Rate Plays

- **Long TLT / IEF** – Duration extension; Treasuries rally as Fed cuts in response to recession
- **Long TIPS (TIP)** – Inflation protection if recession is accompanied by stagflationary dynamics
- **Steepener trade: short 2Y / long 10Y** – Curve steepens aggressively as Fed cuts short rates; expressed via Treasury futures

### Currency & Volatility Hedges

- **Long USD (UUP)** – US dollar typically strengthens in global risk-off recessions
- **Long JPY** – Traditional safe-haven currency appreciates in risk-off environments
- **Long VIX calls or VIX call spreads** – Recession onset causes sharp VIX spikes; 60–90 day tenor
- **Long UVXY** – Leveraged VIX ETF for short-term event hedges (decay makes this a short-term trade only)

## Scenario-Based Positioning

### Scenario A: Soft Landing / Slowdown (Level 2)

**Probability driver:** GDP growth <1%, mild increase in unemployment, Fed pausing rate hikes

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Trim cyclicals (industrials, consumer discretionary) 5% | Reduce exposure to earnings-at-risk sectors |
| Add XLP, XLV 5% | Begin building defensive allocation |
| Add IEF 5% | Intermediate-duration bonds; partial rate cut benefit |
| Add GLD 3–5% | Early safe-haven positioning |
| Maintain equity core, reduce leverage | Slowdown ≠ recession; avoid over-hedging |

### Scenario B: Mild Recession (Level 3)

**Probability driver:** Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP, unemployment rising above 5%, earnings estimates cut broadly

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Increase XLP, XLV, XLU to 15–20% combined | Core defensive positioning |
| Add TLT 10% | Rate cut cycle begins; long duration benefits |
| Reduce or eliminate IWM exposure | Small caps most vulnerable |
| Buy SPY put spreads (3–6 month tenor) | Defined-risk tail hedge |
| Short HYG or buy HYG puts | Credit deterioration play |
| Add WMT, COST | Trade-down beneficiaries |

### Scenario C: Deep Recession (Level 4)

**Probability driver:** Unemployment above 7%, credit markets seizing, widespread corporate earnings collapses

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Max defensive allocation (XLP, XLV, XLU) 25–30% | Capital preservation priority |
| Increase TLT to 15–20% | Aggressive rate cuts; flight to safety |
| Add GLD 10% | Deep uncertainty; store of value |
| Exit or heavily hedge financials (KBE, KRE puts) | Loan loss and capital impairment risk |
| Long USD (UUP) | Risk-off dollar strengthening |
| Buy VIX calls (60–90 day) | Volatility hedge through peak distress |
| Target NOBL, VIG for equity exposure | Dividend Aristocrats hold value better in prolonged downturns |

### Scenario D: Recovery / Expansion Re-Entry (Level 1–2)

**Probability driver:** Positive GDP two consecutive quarters, unemployment falling, PMI above 50, Fed pivoting to neutral/accommodative

| Action | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|
| Gradually re-add cyclicals (XLY, XLI, XLK) | Cyclicals lead recovery; early-cycle outperformance |
| Trim TLT; shift to shorter duration | Yields rise as recovery priced in |
| Reduce GLD allocation | Risk-on; safe havens deflate |
| Add small caps (IWM) selectively | Small caps typically outperform early in recovery |
| Re-add financials (XLF) | Banks benefit from rising loan demand and NIM expansion |
| Unwind SPY hedges | Hedge cost not justified in recovery |

## Key Indicators & Triggers

### Early Warning Signals (Begin Defensive Positioning)
- Yield curve inversion (10Y/2Y or 10Y/3M) sustained for 3+ months
- ISM Manufacturing PMI below 48 for two consecutive months
- Initial jobless claims trend rising above 250,000 weekly for a month
- Conference Board Leading Economic Index (LEI) declining for 6+ consecutive months
- Consumer confidence dropping more than 10 points in a single month
- High-yield credit spreads widening more than 150bps in 30 days

### Recession Confirmation Signals (Full Defensive Positioning)
- BEA advance GDP estimate negative for two consecutive quarters
- Unemployment rate rising 0.5%+ from cycle low (Sahm Rule trigger)
- NBER official recession declaration
- Federal Reserve initiating emergency rate cuts
- Major bank earnings guidance sharply reduced due to loan loss provisioning

### Recovery / Re-Entry Signals (Begin Rotating to Cyclicals)
- ISM Manufacturing PMI recovering above 50 for two consecutive months
- Initial jobless claims trending lower for 6+ consecutive weeks
- Yield curve steepening (short end falling faster than long end)
- S&P 500 earnings growth turning positive year-over-year
- Federal Reserve language shifting toward neutral from accommodative
